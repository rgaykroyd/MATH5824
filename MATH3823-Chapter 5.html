5 Modelling proportions - the logistic model

5.1 The binomial distribution

The Binomial distribution Bin⁢(m,p)\text{Bin}(m,p) is the distribution of the number of successes in mm independent trials, where pp is the probability of success in each trial. For example, tossing a fair coin 100 times would result in a Bin⁢(100,0.5)\text{Bin}(100,0.5) distribution for the number of ’heads’. The term “success” need not correspond to a favourable outcome; it is merely the language traditionally used by statisticians in connection with this model. For example, “success” might correspond to death.

If yy has a Bin⁢(m,p)\text{Bin}(m,p) distribution, its pmf is

f⁢(y)\displaystyle f(y) =(my)⁢py⁢(1-p)m-y.\displaystyle=\left(\begin{array}[]{c}m\\ y\end{array}\right)p^{y}(1-p)^{m-y}. (5.1)

The special case with m=1m=1 is the Bernoulli distribution. This is the distribution of the probability of success in a single trial. Suppose we conduct mm independent Bernoulli trails, each having success probability pp. Let BjB_{j} denote the result of the jjth Bernoulli trial, where Bj=1B_{j}=1 if the jjth trial results in success, and let Bj=0B_{j}=0 otherwise. Then the total number of successes has a Binomial distribution:

y\displaystyle y =∑j=1mBj\displaystyle=\sum_{j=1}^{m}B_{j}
∼Bin⁢(m,p).\displaystyle\sim\text{Bin}(m,p). (5.2)

We consider data where each observation yiy_{i} originates as a sum of mim_{i} i.i.d. Bernoulli random variables as in (5.2), where for each ii the success probability pip_{i} may differ due to the influence of covariates xix_{i}. Thus

yi\displaystyle y_{i} ∼Bin⁢(mi,pi),for ⁢i=1,…,n,\displaystyle\sim\text{Bin}(m_{i},p_{i}),\qquad\text{for~{}}i=1,\ldots,n, (5.3)

where pip_{i} depends on xi\/x_{i}.

5.3 Residuals

The Pearson residuals for Binomial data take the form:

eiP\displaystyle e^{P}_{i} =\displaystyle= yi-mi⁢p^imi⁢p^i⁢(1-p^i).\displaystyle\frac{y_{i}-m_{i}\hat{p}_{i}}{\sqrt{m_{i}\hat{p}_{i}(1-\hat{p}_{i% })}}.

For large mim_{i}, the usual Normal approximation to the Binomial means that the Pearson residuals are approximately N⁢(0,1)N(0,1) distributed.

5.5 Overdispersion

Examination of residuals and deviances may indicate that a model is not an adequate fit to the data. One possible reason is overdispersion. This can occur for any error distribution where the variance is linked to the mean — e.g. Binomial, Poisson. Overdispersion that occurs with these distributions is called extra-Binomial or extra-Poisson variation.

Recall that if yi∼Bin⁢(mi,pi)y_{i}\sim\text{Bin}(m_{i},p_{i}), Var⁢(yi)=mi⁢pi⁢(1-pi)\mathrm{Var}(y_{i})=m_{i}p_{i}(1-p_{i}). Overdispersion occurs if observations which have been modelled by a Bin⁢(mi,p^i)\text{Bin}(m_{i},\widehat{p}_{i}) distribution have substantially greater variation than mi⁢p^i⁢(1-p^i)m_{i}\widehat{p}_{i}(1-\widehat{p}_{i}). This will lead to a value of DD substantially greater than the expected value of n-rn-r. This can occur if the model is missing appropriate explanatory variables or has the wrong link function, or if the yiy_{i} are not independent.

One solution is to include an extra parameter τ\tau in the model so that Var⁢(yi)=mi⁢pi⁢(1-pi)⁢τ\mathrm{Var}(y_{i})=m_{i}p_{i}(1-p_{i})\tau. For more details, see Section 7.7 of Dobson or chapter 6 of Collett (1991) “Modelling Binary data”, Chapman & Hall.

The glm function in R allows for extra-Binomial or extra-Poisson variation through setting family=quasibinomial() or family=quasipoisson().
